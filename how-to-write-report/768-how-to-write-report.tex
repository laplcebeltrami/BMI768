\subsection{Validation}

The validation is designed to establish not only overall performance but also robustness under different error regimes, with particular emphasis on false positives and false negatives. All procedures will be described in sufficient detail, including simulation generation, preprocessing, parameter choices, and evaluation metrics, to ensure full reproducibility by independent researchers. Validation with ground truth will be performed using simulated data where the underlying structure is explicitly known. This allows direct quantification of how accurately the proposed method recovers the true signal or structure of interest. To assess false positives, we will consider settings in which the target structure is absent and evaluate whether the method incorrectly detects spurious patterns or signals. To assess false negatives, we will construct scenarios where the target structure is present and evaluate the extent to which the method fails to detect or recover it. These two complementary settings provide a principled way to characterize sensitivity and specificity of the method. 


Comparisons will be conducted against appropriate baseline methods that represent existing or standard approaches to the same problem. These baselines will be implemented under comparable conditions, including consistent data representations, preprocessing steps, and evaluation criteria. Performance will be evaluated using metrics aligned with the scientific objective, including reconstruction error, statistical significance, and clustering accuracy. The comparison framework will be explicitly defined to ensure that any observed improvements can be attributed to the methodological contributions rather than differences in implementation. For real data, validation will focus on reproducibility, stability, and interpretability. This includes assessing consistency across repeated measurements, robustness to noise or subsampling, and agreement with known or expected patterns. Together, the simulation-based and real-data validation provide complementary evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed method.

A well-constructed example of the validation framework is provided in the link \footnote{\url{https://github.com/laplcebeltrami/BMI768/blob/main/clustering/SIMULATION_clustering.mlx}}, which reproduces simulation studies from \citep{chung.2023.NI, chung.2024.PLOS} and demonstrates systematic comparisons across multiple baseline methods under both false positive and false negative settings.  



\paragraph{What is considered as baseline?} Baseline methods are any established, widely accepted, or conceptually natural approach that addresses the same task under comparable assumptions, and serves as a reference point for evaluating improvement in accuracy, stability, interpretability, or computational efficiency. In rigorous methodological work, baselines should {\it not} be arbitrary alternatives but must represent meaningful widely accepted competing paradigms that reflect how a practitioner would reasonably solve the problem without the proposed method. The role of the baseline is therefore not merely comparative but interpretive: it clarifies what specific advantage is gained by introducing the proposed new method.

In the context of spectral expansion using the Hodge Laplacian \citep{anand.2023.TMI}, several natural baselines arise depending on the level of structure being exploited. At the most basic level, one may consider generic orthonormal bases constructed through {\it Gram–Schmidt orthogonalization}. While mathematically valid, such bases are agnostic to the underlying topology and do not encode geometric or combinatorial structure. As a result, they provide a useful null comparison demonstrating what is lost when no structural prior is imposed. A more meaningful and commonly accepted baseline is {\it principal component analysis (PCA)}, which constructs an orthonormal basis adapted to variance maximization. PCA captures dominant modes of variation in the data but ignores higher-order interactions encoded in simplicial complexes, such as cyclic flows or higher-dimensional cavities. Comparing against PCA therefore isolates the contribution of topology beyond second-order covariance structure.

Stronger baselines should include graph Laplacian based spectral methods, such as eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian \citep{chung.2019.BNA}. These methods incorporate pairwise relationships and are widely used in medical imaging and network analysis. However, they fundamentally operate on node signals (0-forms) and cannot distinguish between gradient and curl components of edge flows. In contrast, the Hodge Laplacian explicitly decomposes edge flows into gradient, curl, and harmonic components, enabling separation of local fluctuations from global topological structure. Thus, comparison with graph Laplacian methods directly demonstrates the benefit of modeling higher-order interactions and enforcing topological consistency.
